So the only operations that can be performed at that lowest level are those that <em>operate on single-bit values</em> (just ones and zeros, that is, just ons and offs). These are called <em>logical (or Boolean) functions</em>. (They're predicates, because their range is Booleans, but these are special in that their <em>domain</em> is also Booleans.) There are three Boolean functions in the Operators palette:<br /> <img class="indent" src="/img/prog/Booleans.png" alt="and, or, not blocks" title="and, or, not blocks" /><br /> <div class="comment">AAP-2.F.5, AAP-2.F.1</div> Notice that both the blocks themselves and the input slots in the blocks are hexagonal. Boolean functions take Boolean values (<code>True</code> or <code>False</code>) as inputs and report a new Boolean value as output. When you use Boolean functions in a program, though, the inputs will usually be expressions such as <img src="/img/2-complexity/var=3.png" alt="variable = 3" title="variable = 3" />. </p> <div class="forYouToDo"> <ol> <li> Experiment with different input values in all three blocks, and take notes about the results you get. <div class="endnote"> <p>Instead of dragging a <code>true</code> or <code>false</code> block into the input slot of an <code>and</code>, <code>or</code>, or <code>not</code> block, you can click the empty input slot to control a <code>true</code>/<code>false</code> toggle: <img class="inline" src="/img/2-complexity/true-and-false.png" alt="(true) and (false)" title="(true) and (false)" /></p> </div> </li> </ol> </div> <p> The <code>and</code> and <code>not</code> blocks work exactly the way you'd expect from the meanings of those words in English: <ul> <div class="comment">AAP-2.F.3</div> <li><img src="/img/2-complexity/true-and-true.png" alt="(true) and (true)" title="(true) and (true)" /> reports <code>true</code>, and any other combination of inputs reports <code>false</code>.</li> <div class="comment">AAP-2.F.2</div> <li><img src="/img/blocks/not.png" alt="not ()" title="not ()" /> reports the opposite of whichever input value you use.</li> </ul> <div class="comment">AAP-2.F.4</div> But <code>or</code> is a little different in computer science. In English, the word "or" has two different meanings: <ul> <li><strong>Either but not both:</strong> such as "Let's check whether it will be rainy or sunny tomorrow." In computer science, this is called <em>exclusive or</em> because one option excludes the other.</li> <li><strong>Either or both:</strong> such as "Ask your teacher if you have any questions or if you need help." This is called <em>inclusive or</em> because it's possible for both options to be included.</li> </ul> </p> <div class="forYouToDo"> <ol start="2"> <li>Experiment.
